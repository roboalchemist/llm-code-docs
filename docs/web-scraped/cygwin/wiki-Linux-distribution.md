From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Operating system based on the Linux kernel

[![Image 4](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Ubuntu_25.10_default_desktop_-_English.png/500px-Ubuntu_25.10_default_desktop_-_English.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Ubuntu_25.10_default_desktop_-_English.png)

[Ubuntu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ubuntu "Ubuntu"), one of the most popular desktop Linux distributions

A **Linux distribution**,[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-1) often abbreviated as **distro**, is an [operating system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operating_system "Operating system") that includes the [Linux kernel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_kernel "Linux kernel") for its [kernel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kernel_(operating_system) "Kernel (operating system)") functionality. Although the name does not imply [product distribution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distribution_(marketing) "Distribution (marketing)") per se, a distro—if distributed on its own—is often obtained via a [website](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Website "Website") intended specifically for the purpose. Distros have been designed for a wide variety of systems ranging from [personal computers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Personal_computer "Personal computer") (for example, [Linux Mint](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_Mint "Linux Mint")) to [servers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Server_(computing) "Server (computing)") (for example, [Red Hat Enterprise Linux](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_Hat_Enterprise_Linux "Red Hat Enterprise Linux")) and from [embedded devices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Embedded_device "Embedded device") (for example, [OpenWrt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OpenWrt "OpenWrt")) to [supercomputers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supercomputer "Supercomputer") (for example, [Rocks Cluster Distribution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rocks_Cluster_Distribution "Rocks Cluster Distribution")).

A distro typically includes many components in addition to the Linux kernel. Commonly, it includes a [package manager](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Package_manager "Package manager"), an [init system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Init "Init") (such as [systemd](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Systemd "Systemd"), [OpenRC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OpenRC "OpenRC"), [SysVinit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SysVinit "SysVinit"), or [runit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Runit "Runit")), [GNU](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/GNU "GNU") tools and [libraries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Library_(computing) "Library (computing)"), documentation, [IP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internet_Protocol "Internet Protocol") network configuration utilities, the [getty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Getty_(Unix) "Getty (Unix)") TTY setup program, and many more. To provide a desktop experience (most commonly the [Mesa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mesa_(computer_graphics) "Mesa (computer graphics)") userspace graphics drivers) a [display server](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Windowing_system "Windowing system") (the most common being the [X.org Server](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X.org_Server "X.org Server"), or, more recently, a [Wayland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wayland_(display_server_protocol) "Wayland (display server protocol)") compositor such as [Sway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sway_(window_manager) "Sway (window manager)"), [KDE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/KDE "KDE")'s [KWin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/KWin "KWin"), or [GNOME](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/GNOME "GNOME")'s [Mutter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mutter_(software) "Mutter (software)")), a [desktop environment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Desktop_environment "Desktop environment") (most commonly [GNOME](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/GNOME "GNOME"), [KDE Plasma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/KDE_Plasma "KDE Plasma"), or [Xfce](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xfce "Xfce")), a [sound server](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sound_server "Sound server") (usually either [PulseAudio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PulseAudio "PulseAudio") or more recently [PipeWire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PipeWire "PipeWire")), and other related programs may be included or installed by the user.

Typically, most of the included software is [free and open-source software](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_and_open-source_software "Free and open-source software") – made available both as [binary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Executable "Executable") for convenience and as [source code](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Source_code "Source code") to allow for modifying it. A distro may also include [proprietary software](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proprietary_software "Proprietary software") that is not available in source code form, such as a [device driver](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Device_driver "Device driver")[binary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_blob "Binary blob").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-x3fEd-2)

A distro may be described as a particular assortment of application and utility software (various GNU tools and libraries, for example), packaged with the Linux kernel in such a way that its capabilities meet users' needs.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-Rn9ZU-3) The software is usually adapted to the distribution and then combined into software packages by the distribution's maintainers. The software packages are available online in [repositories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Software_repository "Software repository"), which are storage locations usually distributed around the world.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-IpPUW-4)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-Eg74B-5) Beside "glue" components, such as the distribution installers (for example, [Debian-Installer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Debian-Installer "Debian-Installer") and [Anaconda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anaconda_(installer) "Anaconda (installer)")) and the package management systems, very few packages are actually written by a distribution's maintainers.

Distributions have been designed for a wide range of computing environments, including [desktops](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Desktop_computer "Desktop computer"), [servers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Server_(computing) "Server (computing)"), [laptops](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laptop "Laptop"), [netbooks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Netbook "Netbook"), [mobile devices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mobile_device "Mobile device") (phones and tablets),[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-mOuND-6)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-3PAkr-7) and [embedded systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Embedded_systems "Embedded systems").[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-huumN-8)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-wrjjJ-9) There are commercially backed distributions, such as [Red Hat Enterprise Linux](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_Hat_Enterprise_Linux "Red Hat Enterprise Linux") ([Red Hat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_Hat "Red Hat")), [openSUSE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OpenSUSE "OpenSUSE") ([SUSE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SUSE_S.A. "SUSE S.A.")) and [Ubuntu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ubuntu "Ubuntu") ([Canonical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canonical_(company) "Canonical (company)")), and entirely community-driven distributions, such as [Debian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Debian "Debian"), [Slackware](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slackware "Slackware"), [Gentoo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gentoo_Linux "Gentoo Linux") and [Arch Linux](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arch_Linux "Arch Linux"). Most distributions come ready-to-use and prebuilt for a specific [instruction set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Instruction_set "Instruction set"), while some (such as [Gentoo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gentoo_Linux "Gentoo Linux")) are distributed mostly in [source code](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Source_code "Source code") form and must be [built](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Software_build "Software build") before installation.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-HSFsG-10)

History
-------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Linux_distribution&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: History")]

[![Image 5](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Linux_0_12.jpg/250px-Linux_0_12.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Linux_0_12.jpg)

5.25-inch [floppy disks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Floppy_disk "Floppy disk") holding a very early version of Linux

[![Image 6: Timeline of Linux distributions](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Linux_Distribution_Timeline.svg/250px-Linux_Distribution_Timeline.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Linux_Distribution_Timeline.svg)

Timeline of the development of main Linux distributions[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-tGGs6-11)

[Linus Torvalds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linus_Torvalds "Linus Torvalds") developed the [Linux kernel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_kernel "Linux kernel") and distributed its first version, 0.01, in 1991. Linux was initially distributed as [source code](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Source_code "Source code") only, and later as a pair of downloadable [floppy disk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Floppy_disk "Floppy disk") images: one bootable and containing the Linux kernel itself, and the other with a set of GNU utilities and tools for setting up a file system. Since the installation procedure was complicated, especially in the face of growing amounts of available software, distributions sprang up to simplify it.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-MhhmD-12)

Early distributions included:

*   Torvalds' "_Boot-Root_" images, from v0.95a onwards "_Root_" was maintained by Jim Winstead Jr., the aforementioned disk image pair with the kernel and the absolute minimal tools to get started (0.10: 4 November 1991)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-13)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-14)[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-15)[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-ovoFu-16)
*   [MCC Interim Linux](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MCC_Interim_Linux "MCC Interim Linux") (3 March 1992)[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-17)
*   [TAMU](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Texas_A%26M_University "Texas A&M University") Linux, based on MCC with simpler installation and X11 (14 July 1992)[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-18)
*   [Softlanding Linux System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Softlanding_Linux_System "Softlanding Linux System") (SLS) which included the X Window System and was the most comprehensive distribution for a short time (15 August 1992)[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-19)
*   [H.J. Lu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HJ_Lu "HJ Lu")'s "_bootable rootdisks_" (23 September 1992),[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-20)[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-21) and "_Linux Base System_" (5 October 1992)[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-22)[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-23)
*   [Yggdrasil Linux/GNU/X](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yggdrasil_Linux/GNU/X "Yggdrasil Linux/GNU/X"), a commercial distribution (alpha: 8 December 1992)

The two oldest, still active distribution projects started in 1993. The SLS distribution was not well maintained, so in July 1993 a new SLS-based distribution, Slackware, was released by [Patrick Volkerding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patrick_Volkerding "Patrick Volkerding").[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-oOaPr-24) Also dissatisfied with SLS, Ian Murdock set to create a free distribution by founding [Debian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Debian "Debian") in August 1993, with first public BETA released in January 1994 and first stable version in June 1996.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-25)[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-0qLCM-26)

Users were attracted to Linux distributions as alternatives to MS-DOS compatible operating systems, Windows, Classic Mac OS, and [proprietary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proprietary_software "Proprietary software") versions of [Unix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unix "Unix"). Most early adopters were familiar with Unix from work or school. They embraced Linux distributions for their low (or absent) cost, and the availability of the [source code](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Source_code "Source code") for most or all of their software.

As of 2025, Linux has become more popular in server and embedded devices markets than in the desktop market. It is used in approximately 64.5% of web servers;[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-w8AEo-27) its current operating system market share is about 2.51%,[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-Ay5cT-28) and its desktop operating system market share is about 2.94%.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-Desktop_OS_Market_Share-29)

Components
----------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Linux_distribution&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Components")]

[![Image 7](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Linux_kernel_ubiquity.svg/250px-Linux_kernel_ubiquity.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Linux_kernel_ubiquity.svg)

A Linux distribution is usually built around a [package management system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Package_management_system "Package management system"), which puts together the [Linux kernel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_kernel "Linux kernel"), free and open-source software, and occasionally some proprietary software.

Many Linux distributions provide an installation system akin to that provided with other modern operating systems. Other distributions, including [Gentoo Linux](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gentoo_Linux "Gentoo Linux"), provide only the binaries of a basic kernel, compilation tools, and an installer; the installer compiles all the requested software for the specific [architecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CPU_Architecture "CPU Architecture") of the user's computer, using these tools and the software's source code.

### Package management

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Linux_distribution&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Package management")]

See also: [Package management system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Package_management_system "Package management system") and [Linux package formats](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_package_formats "Linux package formats")

Distributions are normally segmented into _packages_. Each package contains a specific application or service. Examples of packages are a library for handling the [PNG](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Portable_Network_Graphics "Portable Network Graphics") image format, a collection of fonts, and a [web browser](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Web_browser "Web browser").

The package is typically provided as compiled code, with installation and removal of packages handled by a [package management system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Package_management_system "Package management system") (PMS) rather than a simple [file archiver](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File_archiver "File archiver"). Each package intended for such a PMS contains meta-information such as its description, version number, and its [dependencies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dependency_(computer_science) "Dependency (computer science)") (other packages it requires to run). The package management system evaluates this meta-information to allow package searches, perform automatic upgrades to newer versions, and to check that all dependencies of a package are present (and either notify the user to install them, or install them automatically). The package can also be provided as [source code](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Source_code "Source code") to be compiled on the system.

Most distributions install packages, including the kernel and other core operating system components, in a predetermined configuration. A few now require or permit configuration adjustments at first install time. This makes installation less daunting, particularly for new users, but is not always acceptable. For specific requirements, much software must be carefully configured to be useful, to work correctly with other software, or to be secure, and local administrators are often obliged to spend time reviewing and reconfiguring it.

Some (but not all) distributions go to considerable lengths to adjust and customize the software they include, and some provide configuration tools to help users do so.

By obtaining and installing _everything_ normally provided in a distribution, an administrator may create a "distribution less" installation. It is possible to build such systems from scratch, avoiding distributions altogether. One needs a way to generate the first binaries until the system is _[self-hosting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-hosting\_(compilers) "Self-hosting (compilers)")_. This can be done via compilation on another system capable of building binaries for the intended target (possibly by [cross-compilation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross-compilation "Cross-compilation")). For example, see [Linux From Scratch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_From_Scratch "Linux From Scratch").

Types and trends
----------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Linux_distribution&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Types and trends")]

Further information: [Linux adoption](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_adoption "Linux adoption") and [Comparison of Linux distributions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comparison_of_Linux_distributions "Comparison of Linux distributions")

In broad terms, Linux distributions may be:

*   Commercial or non-commercial
*   Designed for enterprise users, [power users](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_user "Power user"), or for home users
*   Supported on multiple types of hardware, or platform-specific, even to the extent of certification by the platform vendor
*   Designed for servers, desktops, or embedded devices
*   General purpose or highly specialized toward specific machine functionalities (e.g. firewalls, network routers, and [computer clusters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_cluster "Computer cluster"))
*   Targeted at specific user groups, for example through language [internationalization and localization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internationalization_and_localization "Internationalization and localization"), or through inclusion of many [music production](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Music_production "Music production") or [scientific computing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_computing "Scientific computing") packages
*   Built primarily for [security](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_security "Computer security"), [usability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Usability "Usability"), portability, or comprehensiveness
*   Standard release or [rolling release](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rolling_release "Rolling release"), see below
*   Mutable release (the [root](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Root_directory "Root directory") file system is read-write capable) or immutable release (the root file system is read-only)

The diversity of Linux distributions is due to technical, organizational, and philosophical variation among vendors and users. The permissive licensing of free software means that users with sufficient knowledge and interest can customize any extant distribution, or design one to suit their own needs.

### Rolling distributions compared to standard releases

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Linux_distribution&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Rolling distributions compared to standard releases")]

Rolling Linux distributions are kept current using small and frequent [updates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patch_(computing) "Patch (computing)"). The terms _partially rolling_ and _partly rolling_ (along with synonyms _semi-rolling_ and _half-rolling_), _fully rolling_, _truly rolling_ and _optionally rolling_ are sometimes used by software developers and users.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-VkQMs-30)[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-Jzsjg-31)[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-pThSN-32)[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-jMxHl-33)[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-rVo8I-34)[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-XqSOX-35)

Repositories of **rolling distributions** usually contain very recent [software releases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Software_release "Software release")—often the latest stable versions available.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-pThSN-32) They have pseudo-releases and installation media that are simply [snapshots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Revision_control#Baselines_labels_and_tags "Revision control") of the distribution at the time of the installation image's release. Typically, a rolling-release OS installed from older installation medium can be fully updated after it is installed.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-pThSN-32)[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-36)

Depending on the usage case, there can be pros and cons to both standard release and rolling release [software development methodologies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Software_development_methodologies "Software development methodologies").[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-Perrin-37)

In terms of the [software development process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Software_development_process "Software development process"), **standard releases** require significant development effort to keep old versions up-to-date by propagating [bug](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Software_bug "Software bug") fixes back to the newest branch, versus focusing on the newest [development branch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Development_branch "Development branch"). Also, unlike rolling releases, standard releases require more than one code branch to be developed and maintained, which increases the workload of the software developers and maintainers.

On the other hand, software features and technology planning are easier in standard releases due to a better understanding of upcoming features in the next version(s). [Software release cycles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Software_release_cycle "Software release cycle") can also be synchronized with those of major [upstream](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Upstream_(software_development) "Upstream (software development)") software projects, such as [desktop environments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Desktop_environment "Desktop environment").

As for the [user experience](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/User_experience "User experience"), standard releases are often viewed as more stable and bug-free since software conflicts can be more easily addressed and the software stack more thoroughly tested and evaluated, during the software development cycle.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-Perrin-37)[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-Mandla-38) For this reason, they tend to be the preferred choice in enterprise environments and mission-critical tasks.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-Perrin-37)

However, rolling releases offer more current software which can also provide increased stability and fewer software bugs along with the additional benefits of new features, greater functionality, faster running speeds, and improved system and [application security](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Application_security "Application security"). Regarding [software security](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Software_security "Software security"), the rolling release model can have advantages in timely security updates, fixing system or application [security bugs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Security_bug "Security bug") and [vulnerabilities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vulnerability_(computing) "Vulnerability (computing)"), that standard releases may have to wait till the next release for or patch in various versions. In a rolling release distribution, where the user has chosen to run it as a highly dynamic system, the constant flux of software packages can introduce new unintended vulnerabilities.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-Perrin-37)

Installation-free distributions (live CD/USB)
---------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Linux_distribution&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Installation-free distributions (live CD/USB)")]

Main articles: [Live CD](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Live_CD "Live CD") and [Live USB](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Live_USB "Live USB")

A "live" distribution is a Linux distribution that can be booted from removable storage media such as [optical discs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Optical_disc "Optical disc") or [USB flash drives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/USB_flash_drive "USB flash drive"), instead of being installed on and booted from a [hard disk drive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hard_disk_drive "Hard disk drive"). The portability of installation-free distributions makes them advantageous for applications such as demonstrations, borrowing someone else's computer, rescue operations, or as installation media for a standard distribution.

When the operating system is booted from a read-only medium such as a CD or DVD, any user data that needs to be retained between sessions cannot be stored on the boot device but must be written to another storage device, such as a USB flash drive or a hard disk drive.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-9y8MA-39)

Many Linux distributions provide a "live" form in addition to their conventional form, which is a network-based or removable-media image intended to be used only for installation; such distributions include [antiX](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AntiX "AntiX"), [SUSE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SUSE_Linux "SUSE Linux"), Ubuntu, [Linux Mint](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_Mint "Linux Mint"), [MX Linux](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MX_Linux "MX Linux") and [Fedora Linux](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fedora_Linux "Fedora Linux"). Some distributions, including [Knoppix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knoppix "Knoppix"), [Puppy Linux](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Puppy_Linux "Puppy Linux"), Devil-Linux, [SuperGamer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SuperGamer "SuperGamer"), [SliTaz GNU/Linux](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SliTaz_GNU/Linux "SliTaz GNU/Linux") and [dyne:bolic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dyne:bolic "Dyne:bolic"), are designed primarily for live use. Additionally, some minimal distributions can be run directly from as little space as one floppy disk without the need to change the contents of the system's hard disk drive.[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-TBjY5-40)

### Widely used GNU-based or GNU-compatible distributions

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Linux_distribution&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Widely used GNU-based or GNU-compatible distributions")]

*   [Debian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Debian "Debian"), a non-commercial distribution and one of the earliest, maintained by a volunteer developer community with a strong commitment to free software principles and democratic project management. 
    *   [Ubuntu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ubuntu "Ubuntu"), a desktop and server distribution derived from Debian, maintained by British company [Canonical Ltd.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canonical_(company) "Canonical (company)")

        *   [Linux Mint](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_Mint "Linux Mint"), a distribution based on and compatible with Ubuntu. Supports multiple desktop environments, among others GNOME Shell [fork](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fork_(software_development) "Fork (software development)")[Cinnamon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cinnamon_(desktop_environment) "Cinnamon (desktop environment)") and [GNOME 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/GNOME_2 "GNOME 2") fork [MATE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MATE_(desktop_environment) "MATE (desktop environment)").

### Linux-kernel-based operating systems

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Linux_distribution&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Linux-kernel-based operating systems")]

Several operating systems include the Linux kernel, but have a userland that differs significantly from that of mainstream Linux distributions:

*   [Android](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Android_(operating_system) "Android (operating system)"), [Google](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Google "Google")'s commercial operating system based on [Android OSP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Android_(operating_system) "Android (operating system)") that runs on many devices such as smartphones, smart TVs, set-top boxes. 
    *   There are several third-party distributions of AOSP, in turn, including: [LineageOS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LineageOS "LineageOS"), [GrapheneOS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/GrapheneOS "GrapheneOS") and [Android-x86](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Android-x86 "Android-x86").

*   [ChromeOS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ChromeOS "ChromeOS"), Google's commercial operating system based on [ChromiumOS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ChromiumOS "ChromiumOS") that only runs on [Chromebooks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chromebook "Chromebook"), [Chromeboxes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chromebox "Chromebox") and [tablet computers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tablet_computer "Tablet computer"). Like Android, it has the [Google Play Store](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Google_Play "Google Play") and other [Google apps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Google_Workspace "Google Workspace"). Support for applications that require GNU compatibility is available through a virtual machine called Crostini and referred to by Google as Linux support, see [Chromebook §Compatibility with Linux applications (GNU compatibility)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chromebook#Compatibility_with_Linux_applications_(GNU_compatibility) "Chromebook").

Whether such operating systems count as a "Linux distribution" is a controversial topic. They use the Linux kernel, so the [Linux Foundation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_Foundation "Linux Foundation")[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-BDtXs-42) and [Chris DiBona](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chris_DiBona "Chris DiBona"),[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-Snohr-43) Google's former open-source chief, agree that Android is a Linux distribution; others, such as Google engineer Patrick Brady, disagree by noting the lack of support for many GNU tools in Android, including [glibc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glibc "Glibc").[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-59omI-44)

Other Linux-kernel-based operating systems include [Tizen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tizen "Tizen"), [Mer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mer_(software_distribution) "Mer (software distribution)")/[Sailfish OS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sailfish_OS "Sailfish OS"), [KaiOS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/KaiOS "KaiOS") and [Amazon's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amazon_(company) "Amazon (company)")[Kindle firmware](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amazon_Kindle "Amazon Kindle").

Interdistribution issues
------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Linux_distribution&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Interdistribution issues")]

The [Free Standards Group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_Standards_Group "Free Standards Group") was an organization formed by major software and hardware vendors that aims to improve interoperability between different distributions. Among their proposed standards are the [Linux Standard Base](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_Standard_Base "Linux Standard Base"), which defines a common [ABI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Application_binary_interface "Application binary interface") and packaging system for Linux, and the [Filesystem Hierarchy Standard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Filesystem_Hierarchy_Standard "Filesystem Hierarchy Standard") which recommends a standard file naming chart, notably the basic directory names found on the root of the tree of any Linux filesystem. Those standards, however, see limited use, even among the distributions developed by members of the organization.[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-47)

The diversity of Linux distributions means that not all software runs on all distributions, depending on what libraries and other system attributes are required. [Packaged software](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_package_formats "Linux package formats") and software repositories are usually specific to a particular distribution, though cross-installation is sometimes possible on closely related distributions.[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-48)

One of solutions for interdistribution issues, particularly to run software created for a distribution on another, is by using [container](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Containerization_(computing) "Containerization (computing)") such as [Distrobox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distrobox "Distrobox").[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-LWN-Distrobox-49)

Installation
------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Linux_distribution&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Installation")]

There are several ways to install a Linux distribution. The most popular method of installing Linux is by booting from a live [USB memory stick](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/USB_memory_stick "USB memory stick"), which can be created by using a USB image writer application and the ISO image, which can be downloaded from various Linux distribution websites. DVD disks, CD disks, network installations and even other hard drives can also be used as "installation media".[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-JnYKa-50)

In the 1990s, Linux distributions were installed using sets of [floppy disks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Floppy_disk "Floppy disk"), but this has been abandoned by all major distributions. By the 2000s, many distributions offered CD and DVD sets with the vital packages on the first disk and less important packages on later ones. Some distributions, such as [Debian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Debian "Debian") also enabled installing over a network after booting from either a set of floppy disks or a CD with only a small amount of data on it.[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-Qp3Oj-51)

New users tend to begin by [partitioning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disk_partitioning "Disk partitioning") a hard drive to keep their formerly installed operating system. The Linux distribution can then be installed on its own separate partition without affecting formerly saved data.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-52)

In a Live CD setup, the computer boots the entire operating system from CD without first installing it on the computer's hard disk. Many distributions have a Live CD _installer_, where the computer boots the operating system from the disk, and it can then be installed on the computer's hard disk, providing a seamless transition from the OS running from the CD to the OS running from the hard disk.

Both servers and [personal computers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Personal_computer "Personal computer") that come with Linux already installed are available from vendors including [Hewlett-Packard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hewlett-Packard "Hewlett-Packard"), [Dell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dell "Dell") and [System76](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/System76 "System76").

On embedded devices, Linux is typically held in the device's [firmware](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Firmware "Firmware") and may or may not be consumer-accessible.

[Anaconda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anaconda_installer "Anaconda installer"), one of the more popular installers, is used by [Red Hat Enterprise Linux](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_Hat_Enterprise_Linux "Red Hat Enterprise Linux"), [Fedora](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fedora_(operating_system) "Fedora (operating system)") (which uses the [Fedora Media Writer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fedora_Media_Writer "Fedora Media Writer")) and other distributions to simplify the installation process. Debian, Ubuntu and many others use [Debian-Installer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Debian-Installer "Debian-Installer").

The process of constantly switching between distributions is often referred to as "distro hopping".[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-dCnRp-53)[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-54)[Virtual machine software](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virtual_machine "Virtual machine") such as [VirtualBox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/VirtualBox "VirtualBox") and [VMware Workstation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/VMware_Workstation "VMware Workstation") virtualize hardware allowing users to test live media on a virtual machine without installing to the real system. Some websites like [DistroWatch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DistroWatch "DistroWatch") offer lists of distributions, and link to screenshots of operating systems as a way to get a first impression of various distributions.

### Installation via an existing operating system

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Linux_distribution&action=edit&section=15 "Edit section: Installation via an existing operating system")]

Some distributions let the user install Linux on top of their current system, such as WinLinux (outdated) [WinLinux](https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/WinLinux "fr:WinLinux") or [coLinux](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cooperative_Linux "Cooperative Linux"). Linux is installed to the Windows hard disk partition, and can be started from inside Windows itself.

Virtual machines (such as VirtualBox or VMware Workstation) also make it possible for Linux to be run inside another OS. The VM software simulates a separate computer onto which the Linux system is installed. After installation, the virtual machine can be booted as if it were an independent computer.

Various tools are also available to perform full [dual-boot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual-boot "Dual-boot") installation from extant platforms with no CD, most notably:

*   The (now deprecated) [Wubi installer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wubi_(installer) "Wubi (installer)"), which allows Windows users to download and install Ubuntu or its derivatives into a [File Allocation Table](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File_Allocation_Table "File Allocation Table") ([FAT32](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/FAT32 "FAT32")) or an NT File System ([NTFS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NTFS "NTFS")) partition with no installation CD, allowing users to easily dual boot between either operating system on the same hard drive without losing data. Replaced by [Ubiquity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ubiquity_(software) "Ubiquity (software)").
*   [Win32-loader](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Win32-loader_(Debian) "Win32-loader (Debian)") was in the process of being integrated into official Debian CDs/DVDs but has been discontinued.[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-55) It allowed Windows users to install [Debian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Debian "Debian") without a CD, though it performs a network installation and thereby requires repartitioning[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-lHWXD-56)
*   [UNetbootin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UNetbootin "UNetbootin"), which allows Windows and Linux users to perform similar no-CD network installations for a wide variety of Linux distributions and additionally provides [live USB](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Live_USB "Live USB") creation support

Proprietary software
--------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Linux_distribution&action=edit&section=16 "Edit section: Proprietary software")]

Some specific proprietary software products are not available in any form for Linux. As of September 2015, the [Steam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steam_(service) "Steam (service)") gaming service has over 1,500 games available on Linux, compared to 2,323 games for Mac and 6,500 Windows games.[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-rCjF9-57)[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-CbQ4u-58)[Emulation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emulator "Emulator") and API-translation projects like [Wine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wine_(software) "Wine (software)") and [CrossOver](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CrossOver "CrossOver") make it possible to run non-Linux-based software on Linux systems, either by emulating a proprietary operating system or by translating proprietary API calls (e.g., calls to Microsoft's [Win32](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Win32 "Win32") or [DirectX](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DirectX "DirectX") APIs) into native Linux API calls. A [virtual machine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virtual_machine "Virtual machine") can also be used to run a proprietary OS (like Microsoft Windows) on top of Linux.

OEM contracts
-------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Linux_distribution&action=edit&section=17 "Edit section: OEM contracts")]

![Image 8](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Ambox_current_red_Asia_Australia.svg/60px-Ambox_current_red_Asia_Australia.svg.png)This section needs to be **updated**. Pre-built computers are usually sold with an operating system other than Linux already installed by the [original equipment manufacturer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Original_equipment_manufacturer "Original equipment manufacturer") (OEM). In the case of [IBM PC compatibles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IBM_PC_compatible "IBM PC compatible"), the OS is usually [Microsoft Windows](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microsoft_Windows "Microsoft Windows"); in the case of [Apple](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apple_Inc. "Apple Inc.")'s [Mac](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mac_(computer) "Mac (computer)") computers, it has always been [macOS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MacOS "MacOS"); [Sun Microsystems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun_Microsystems "Sun Microsystems") sold [SPARC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SPARC "SPARC") hardware with [Solaris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solaris_(operating_system) "Solaris (operating system)") installed; [video game consoles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Video_game_console "Video game console") such as the [Xbox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xbox "Xbox"), [PlayStation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PlayStation "PlayStation"), [Wii](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wii "Wii"), and [Nintendo Switch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nintendo_Switch "Nintendo Switch") each have their own proprietary OS. This limits Linux's market share: consumers are unaware that an alternative exists, they must make a conscious effort to use a different operating system, and they must either perform the actual installation themselves, or depend on support from a friend, relative, or computer professional.

However, it is possible to buy hardware with Linux already installed. [Lenovo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lenovo "Lenovo"), [Hewlett-Packard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hewlett-Packard "Hewlett-Packard"), [Dell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dell "Dell"), Affordy,[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-k0WSz-59)[Purism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Purism_(company) "Purism (company)"), [Pine64](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pine64 "Pine64") and [System76](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/System76 "System76") all sell general-purpose Linux laptops.[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-VECM6-60) Custom-order PC manufacturers will also build Linux systems, but possibly with the [Windows key](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Windows_key "Windows key") on the keyboard. [Fixstars Solutions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fixstars_Solutions "Fixstars Solutions") (formerly Terra Soft) sold Macintosh computers and [PlayStation 3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PlayStation_3 "PlayStation 3") consoles with [Yellow Dog Linux](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yellow_Dog_Linux "Yellow Dog Linux") installed.

It is more common to find embedded devices sold with Linux as the default manufacturer-supported OS, including the Linksys [NSLU2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NSLU2 "NSLU2") NAS device, [TiVo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/TiVo "TiVo")'s line of personal video recorders, and Linux-based cellphones (including Android smartphones), PDAs, and portable music players.

The current Microsoft Windows license lets the manufacturer determine the refund policy.[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-Ax80H-61) With prior versions of Windows, it was possible to obtain a refund if the manufacturer failed to provide the refund by litigation in the small claims courts.[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-SDpza-62) On February 15, 1999, a group of Linux users in [Orange County, California](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orange_County,_California "Orange County, California") held a "Windows Refund Day" protest in an attempt to pressure Microsoft into issuing them refunds.[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-TXPbv-63) In France, the Linuxfrench and AFUL (French speaking Libre Software Users' Association) organizations along with free software activist [Roberto Di Cosmo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roberto_Di_Cosmo "Roberto Di Cosmo") started a "Windows Detax" movement,[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-bSaXD-64) which led to a 2006 petition against "racketiciels" (translation: [Racketware](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Racket_(crime) "Racket (crime)")) with 39,415 signatories and the [DGCCRF](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DGCCRF "DGCCRF") branch of the French government filing several complaints against bundled software.

Statistics
----------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Linux_distribution&action=edit&section=18 "Edit section: Statistics")]

There are no official figures on the popularity, adoption, downloads or [installed base](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Installed_base "Installed base") of Linux distributions.

There are also no official figures for the total number of Linux systems,[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-Prashanth_Venkataram-65)[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-66) partly due to the difficulty of quantifying the number of [PCs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Personal_computer "Personal computer") running Linux (see [Desktop Linux adoption](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Desktop_Linux_adoption "Desktop Linux adoption")), since many users [download](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Download "Download") Linux distributions. Hence, the sales figures for Linux systems and commercial Linux distributions indicate a much lower number of Linux systems and level of [Linux adoption](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_adoption "Linux adoption") than is the case; this is mainly due to Linux being [free and open-source software](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_and_open-source_software "Free and open-source software") that can be downloaded [free of charge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_of_charge "Free of charge").[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-Prashanth_Venkataram-65)[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-gK7DI-67) A Linux Counter Project had kept track of a running [guesstimate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guesstimate "Guesstimate") of the number of Linux systems, but did not distinguish between rolling release and standard release distributions. It ceased operation in August 2018, though a few related blog posts were created through October 2018.[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-dAoq7-68)

Desktop usage statistical reports for particular Linux distributions have been collected and published since July 2014[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-y9sXd-69) by the Linux Hardware Project.

[Statcounter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statcounter "Statcounter"), a web traffic analysis company, within the _operating system market share_, showed that the Linux operating systems had, according to them, 3.9% of the worldwide [market share](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Market_share "Market share") in July 2025.[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_distribution#cite_note-70)
